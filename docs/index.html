Why S3 needs a heartbeat

S3 will happily store anything you throw at it, but it won’t tell you whether your data flows are healthy. BucketPulse turns cold inventory metadata into live signals—showing which buckets are active, which prefixes are going quiet, and where data is silently aging out of relevance.

See S3 as a stream, not a graveyard

S3 will store petabytes of data without blinking—but it won’t tell you what’s flowing right now. BucketPulse shows which prefixes are actively receiving events, which have gone quiet, and where data is slowly aging into cold storage. Instead of a static object listing, you get a live view of how your data is actually moving.

Answer the on-call questions in seconds

“Has data stopped flowing?” “Is this prefix still active?” “What changed in the last hour?” BucketPulse turns S3 Metadata Tables into clear freshness and staleness signals so you can answer on-call questions without diving into Athena, CloudWatch, or ad-hoc scripts. The next time something looks off, you’ll know where to look first.

Built entirely on AWS-native services

BucketPulse runs where your data already lives. It’s deployed with CDK and stitched together from AWS-native building blocks—API Gateway, Lambda, DynamoDB, Athena, and S3 Metadata Tables. No agents, sidecars, or extra clusters to manage. You keep full control in your own account, with a UI that’s just a thin lens over services you already trust.

BucketPulse gives you a single lens into how your S3 data is behaving—from high-level bucket health down to individual prefixes and objects. Browse bucket status, inspect prefix freshness and staleness, scan alerts, or dive into inventory with filters instead of hand-written Athena queries.

How BucketPulse works under the hood

BucketPulse sits on top of S3 Metadata Tables and a small set of serverless building blocks. S3 keeps your inventory and journal tables up to date, a scheduled Lambda uses Athena to crunch freshness and staleness metrics, and a lightweight API serves those results to the BucketPulse UI. No agents, no custom daemons—just metadata in, health signals out.

1

Ingest: S3 Metadata Tables

S3 continuously maintains inventory and journal tables for your buckets. BucketPulse reads these managed tables—never your raw objects—as the source of truth for what’s changing.

2

Compute: Athena + Lambda

A scheduled Lambda runs Athena queries over inventory and journal data to compute per-prefix freshness, staleness, age histograms, and anomalies. Results are written into DynamoDB for fast reads.

3

Present: API + UI

API Gateway exposes the aggregated metrics to the BucketPulse UI, which renders bucket lists, prefix health, alerts, and explorer views—all backed by AWS-native services in your own account.
